Texas Forecaster Consensus – Regional View

As of: Tuesday, June 02, 2026, 3:45 PM CDT
What forecasters are thinking and following across broad areas of Texas right now
Texas Panhandle (Amarillo, Lubbock, Canyon)

Forecasters in the Panhandle are focused on an active upper low and destabilizing atmosphere that may produce scattered to widespread showers and storms Tuesday into Wednesday, with a slight severe threat mainly for hail and damaging winds. Rain chances start isolated this afternoon, increase Tuesday night into Wednesday morning, and could be scattered to widespread across the Caprock and western Panhandles if destabilization is sufficient. Highs are near or slightly below average for early June, with mild temperatures in the upper 80s to low 90s, and a cool day possible Wednesday under widespread clouds and rain. The most likely rainfall total for the next several days is on the order of 0.25 to 0.75 inch, with locally higher amounts possible if storms train or slow down, and a flash flood threat if boundaries stall.

North Texas (DFW, Fort Worth, Denton, Waco, Abilene, Big Spring)

In North Texas, forecasters are tracking a hot pattern with triple-digit heat indices and isolated to scattered afternoon/evening storms, especially along and east of I-35. Rain chances are generally 20–40% on a typical day, with a better window for more widespread storms late in the week as Gulf moisture increases and a front interacts with the dryline. Temperatures are in the mid-to-upper 90s for highs, with lows in the upper 60s to mid-70s, and heat index often exceeding 100–105°F. Abilene and Big Spring fall on the western edge of this region, with similar hot, unstable conditions and isolated to scattered storms. The main rainfall window for the next several days is still Friday–Sunday in many forecast discussions.

West Central Texas (San Angelo)

The pattern is hot and humid, with isolated to scattered afternoon/evening storms, and heat index often in the 100–105°F range. Rain chances are similar to North/Central Texas, with a better chance midweek as Gulf moisture increases. Highs are in the mid-to-upper 90s, with lows in the upper 60s to mid-70s.

Central Texas (Austin, Round Rock, San Antonio, Temple, Killeen)

NWS Austin/San Antonio AFD (Latest): Unsettled weather today through the middle of next week with daily shower and storm chances. Strong to marginally severe storms and locally heavy rains possible. Near widespread measurable rain by Sunday across the eastern 3/4 of the area. Above average temperatures most of the week, mild Sunday and Thursday. Quality rainfall amounts over 1/4 inch expected over about 80% of the forecast area. A typical spring cold front arrives today, bringing best chance of rain since Valentine's Day and possibly highest 2-day rainfall amounts so far this year. Combination of front, moderate shear, and CAPE values exceeding 2000 J/kg could lead to large hail and damaging winds [web:97].

Local Forecasters (UT Weather, Spectrum News Austin, LCRA Bob Rose): Rain and storm chances return beginning tonight in a more unstable environment. A weak cold front settles in from the northeast, south-southeast flow increases low-level moisture, and an upper-level low from the Pacific enhances rain/storm chances by late Wednesday into the weekend. Rain chances: 20% tonight, 40% Wednesday, 60% Thursday, 70% Friday, 50% Saturday, 30% Sunday. 7-day corridor total: 0.75 to 1.00 inch. Highs trend from low 90s today to mid 80s Friday under cloud cover and rain. Lightning threat with any thunderstorms all week [page:1]. LCRA Bob Rose forecasts 1–1.5 inch totals through Saturday, with highs 88–90°F Wednesday–Sunday and lows in low/mid-70s.

Gulf Coast (Houston, Galveston, Corpus Christi)

Along the Gulf Coast, forecasters are tracking tropical moisture and the sea breeze, which produce daily rain chances from today through the week, with increasing coverage Friday and Saturday. The pattern is very humid, with feels-like temperatures often in the 100–105°F range, highs in the low-to-mid 90s, and lows in the mid-to-upper 70s. Rain is scattered to numerous, with the best chance of more widespread storms as tropical moisture increases, and the typical June pattern favors daily afternoon/evening storms.

Lower Rio Grande Valley / Brownsville

Forecasters are watching the start of hurricane season (June 1–November 30) with a below-normal Atlantic season expected due to El Niño, but emphasize that one storm can have a major impact. NOAA forecasts 8–14 named storms, including 3–6 hurricanes and 1–3 major hurricanes, with about a 20% chance of a landfalling hurricane along the Gulf Coast from the Florida Panhandle to Brownsville this season. Day-to-day, hot and humid with scattered afternoon thunderstorms. The broader pattern is monitored for any tropical development that could affect the coast. Coastal impacts can still happen fast and one landfalling storm is all it takes.

West Texas (El Paso, Midland, Odessa, Big Bend)

West Texas forecasters are focused on hot, dry conditions with very low humidity and minimal rain, as the Southwest monsoon is not yet fully active. El Paso highs are in the mid-to-upper 90s, up to low 100s, with lows in the mid-60s to low 70s, and very little precipitation expected in early June. The pattern is dominated by clear skies and desert heat, with only isolated afternoon storms possible far west or at higher elevations, and June rainfall totals typically well under 0.5 inch.

Broadly, forecasters across Texas are following a hot, unstable pattern with an active upper low and Gulf moisture, producing daily shower and storm chances from the Panhandle through North/Central Texas and the Gulf Coast, while West Texas remains largely dry and hot and the Lower Rio Grande Valley is at the start of a below-normal but still consequential hurricane season.
